SMO 2018 Senior Round 2 Q5

A friend showed me this SMO problem:

Starting with any $n$-tuple \(R_0\), \(n>1\), of symbols from \(A,B,C\), we define a sequence \(R_0,R_1,R_2,\ldots\), according to the following rule: If \(R_j = (x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)\), then \(R_{j+1} = (y_1,y_2,\ldots,y_n)\), where \(y_i = x_i\) if \(x_i = x_{i+1}\) (taking \(x_{n+1} = x_1\)) and \(y_i\) is the symbol other than \(x_i,x_{i+1}\) if \(x_i \neq x_{i+1}\). Find all positive integers \(n > 1\) for which there exists some integer \(m > 0\) such that \(R_m = R_0\).

Interestingly, the solution is relatively straightforward after one expresses the problem in terms of linear algebra. Here's my take:

Let \(f\) be a function satisfying \(f(x_i,x_{i+1}) = y_i\) for all \(i\). The key insight is that \(f\) can be expressed as a linear combination in \(\F_3\): replacing \(A,B,C\) with the elements \(0,1,2 \in \F_3\), we have \[y_i = 2x_i + 2x_{i+1}\]

(note that addition and multiplication is done in \(\F_3\)). For example,

\begin{align*} 2(0)+2(0) &= 0 \\ 2(2)+2(2) &= 2 \\ 2(1)+2(2) &= 0 \\ 2(0)+2(2) &= 1. \end{align*}

This is a crucial observation, because now we can express the tuple update rule as an \(n \times n\) matrix:

\[\begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 &&& \\ & 2 & 2 && \\ && 2 & 2 & \\ &&& \ddots & \\ 2 &&&& 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{pmatrix}.\]

Let the \(n \times n\) matrix be \(A_n\) (note that its entries are elements of \(\F_3\)). Say that \(A_n\) is $I$-potent if \({A_n}^m\) is the identity matrix \(I\), for some integer \(m > 0\). Note that \(R_m = R_0\) for some integer \(m > 0\) if and only if \(A_n\) is $I$-potent. Furthermore, if \(B_n\) is the row-reduced form of \(A_n\), then \(A_n\) is $I$-potent if and only if \(B_n\) is $I$-potent.

We shall perform row-reduction on \(A_n\) to find \(B_n\):

\begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 &&&& \\ & 2 & 2 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 2 & 2 \\ 2 &&&&& 2 \end{pmatrix} &\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 &&&& \\ & 1 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 1 & 1 \\ 1 &&&&& 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 &&&& \\ & 1 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 1 & 1 \\ & 2 &&&& 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 &&&& \\ & 1 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 1 & 1 \\ && 1 &&& 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &\dots \\ &\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 &&&& \\ & 1 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 1 & 1 \\ &&&& x & 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 &&&& \\ & 1 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 1 & 1 \\ &&&&& 1-x \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*}

It can be seen that \(x = 1\) if \(n\) is even and \(x = 2\) if \(n\) is odd. If \(x = 1\), then the last row is all zeroes, so \(B_n\) and hence \(A_n\) is non-invertible. This means all powers of \(A_n\) are non-invertible, so \(A_n\) cannot be $I$-potent if \(n\) is even. If \(x = 2\), then we can express \(B_n\) as a sum

\[ D + N = \begin{pmatrix} 1 &&&& \\ & 1 &&& \\ && \ddots && \\ &&& 1 & \\ &&&& 2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 &&&& \\ & 0 & 1 &&& \\ &&& \ddots && \\ &&&& 0 & 1 \\ &&&&& 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

For any \(m > 0\), we can find \((D+N)^m\) using the binomial theorem. To find an \(m\) such that \((D+N)^m = I\), we can impose some conditions to simplify our calculations.

Firstly, let \(m\) be divisible by 3. Then the binomial coefficients \({m \choose k}\) for \(0 < k < m\) are divisible by 3. Thus scaling any matrix with entries in \(\F_3\) by these coefficients yields the zero matrix, so \((D+N)^m = D^m + N^m\).

It turns out that \(N\) is nilpotent of degree \(n\), so we can impose the condition that \(m \ge n\), to ensure that \(N^m = O\). Lastly, \(D^m = I\) if and only if \(2^m = 1\), which means \(m\) must be divisible by \(2\).

Clearly there is some integer \(m \ge n\) that is divisible by \(2\) and \(3\), hence \(B_n\) and thus \(A_n\) is $I$-potent if \(n\) is even.

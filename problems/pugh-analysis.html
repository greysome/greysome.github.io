1-12: Some properties of discrete metric spaces

Let \(M\) be a metric space with the discrete metric, or more generally a homeomorph of \(M\).

(a) Prove that every subset of \(M\) is clopen.

It suffices to prove that every subset is open, since after that one can take complements to prove that every subset is closed. If \(A\) is a subset and \(a \in A\) is a point, then clearly \(M_{0.5}(a) = \{a\} \subset A\), so \(A\) is open.

(b) Prove that every function defined on \(M\) is continuous.

This follows easily from (a), since every preimage is an open set.

(c) Which sequences converge in \(M\)?

Let \(\{p_n\} \rightarrow p\) be a convergent sequence. Then in particular, there is an \(N\) such if \(n > N\), then \(d(p_n,p) < 0.5\), or equivalently \(p_n = p\). Thus \(\{p_n\}\) must be eventually constant. Conversely, it is obvious that every eventually constant sequence converges.

1-20: Perimeter of a square vs a circle

Is the perimeter of a square isometric to the circle? Homeomorphic? Explain.

Let \(f\) be an isometry. Fix some point \(p\) on the square perimeter, and let \(q,r\) be distinct points that a distance of \(d\) from \(p\), as shown in the following diagram. Then \(d(fq,fr) = d(q,r)\) if and only if \(\theta = \pi/2\).

But clearly \(\theta\) varies with \(d\), so this is not always the case, meaning \(f\) is not an isometry.

On the other hand, it is intuitive that the perimeter of a square is homeomorphic to the circle.

1-23: Is \(\Q\) homeomorphic to \(\N\)?

Suppose \(f : \N \rightarrow \Q\) be a homeomorphism. Every point \(n \in \N\) is itself an open set, and \(f\) carries open sets to open sets. Thus \(fn\) is an open set, which cannot be true because there are rational numbers arbitrarily close to any given rational number.

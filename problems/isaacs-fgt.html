1A.1: Condition for subgroup of prime index to be normal

Let \(H\) be a subgroup of prime index \(p\) in the finite group \(G\), and suppose that no prime smaller than \(p\) divides \(\bars{G}\). Prove that \(H \triangleleft G\).

Let \(G\) act on left cosets of \(H\) by left multiplication. The kernel of this action is \[\text{core}_G(H) = \bigcap_{g \in G} gHg^{-1},\]

which is normal in \(G\). Let \(K\) denote the quotient group \(G/\text{core}_G(H)\). By the first isomorphism theorem, \(K\) is isomorphic to a subgroup of \(S_p\), so \(\bars{K} \mid p!\).

Since no prime \(< p\) can divide \(\bars{K}\) (otherwise it would contradict the second hypothesis), it follows that \(\bars{K}\) is either \(1\) or \(p\). The former implies that \(\text{core}_G(H) = G\), which is impossible since \(H\) is a proper subgroup. The latter implies that \(\text{core}_G(H) = H\), i.e. there is only one $G$-conjugate of \(H\). This completes the proof.

1A.2: Order of double coset

Given subgroups \(H,K \subseteq G\) and an element \(g \in G\), the set \[HgK = \{hgk \mid h \in H, k \in K\}\] is called an $(H,K)$-double coset. In the case where \(H\) and \(K\) are finite, show that $\bars{HgK} = \bars{H}\bars{K}/\bars{K \cap H^g}.$

Let \(G = H \times K\). Define the map

\begin{align*} G \times HgK &\rightarrow HgK \\ (h,k) \cdot (h'gk') &\mapsto h(h'gk')k^{-1} \end{align*}

This is easily seen to be a transitive group action, so \[\bars{HgK} = \bars{\mathcal{O}_g} = \frac{\bars{G}}{\bars{G_g}} = \frac{\bars{H}\bars{K}}{\bars{G_g}}.\]

If \((h,k) \in G_g\), then \(hgk^{-1} = g\), which can be rewritten as \(k = g^{-1}hg \in H^g\). Conversely, \((h,g^{-1}hg) \cdot g = hg(g^{-1}h^{-1}g) = g\). Thus \(\bars{G_g} = \bars{K \cap H^g}\), yielding the conclusion.

1A.3: Subgroups with coprime indices

Suppose that \(G\) is finite and that \(H,K \subseteq G\) are subgroups.

(a) Show that \(\bars{H : H \cap K} \le \bars{G : K}\), with equality if and only if \(HK = G\).

This follows easily from the fact that \[\bars{H : H \cap K} = \frac{\bars{H}\bars{K}}{\bars{H \cap K}\bars{K}} = \frac{\bars{HK}}{\bars{K}}.\]

(b) If \(\bars{G : H}\) and \(\bars{G : K}\) are coprime, show that \(HK = G\).

Since \(H \cap K\) is a subgroup of both \(H\) and \(K\), it follows that \(\bars{G : H}\) and \(\bars{G : K}\) both divide \(\bars{G : H \cap K}\). But \(\bars{G : H}\) and \(\bars{G : K}\) are coprime, so we have

\begin{align*} \bars{G : H}\bars{G : K} \mid \bars{G : H \cap K} &\implies \frac{\bars{G}^2}{\bars{H}\bars{K}} \mid \frac{\bars{G}}{\bars{H \cap K}} \\ &\implies \bars{G} \mid \frac{\bars{H}\bars{K}}{\bars{H \cap K}} \\ &\implies \bars{HK} = \bars{G}. \end{align*}

1A.4: Sets of the form \(H^xK^y\)

Suppose that \(G = HK\), where \(H\) and \(K\) are subgroups. Show that also \(G = H^xK^y\) for all elements \(x,y \in G\). Deduce that if \(G = HH^x\) for a subgroup \(H\) and an element \(x \in G\), then \(H = G\).

To prove that \(HK = H^xK^y\), it suffices to show that \(AB = A^xB\) for all subgroups \(A,B\) with \(AB = G\). Since \(A^x\) and \(B\) are then permutable, we have \[H^xK = KH^x = K^yH^x = H^xK^y.\]

Note that

\begin{equation} \label{eq1} \tag{*} \bars{H^xK} = \frac{\bars{H^x}\bars{K}}{\bars{K \cap H^x}} = \frac{\bars{H}\bars{K}}{\bars{K \cap H^x}} = \bars{HxK}. \end{equation}

Since \(G = HK\), we can write \(x\) as a product \(h_0k_0\). For all \(h \in H\) and \(k \in K\), we have

\begin{align*} hk &= hh_0^{-1}(h_0k_0)k_0^{-1}k_0 \\ &= hh_0^{-1}xk_0^{-1}k_0 \\ &\in HxK; \end{align*}

on the other hand, \[hxk = (hh_0)(k_0k) \in HK.\]

Thus \(HxK = HK = G\), which by \eqref{eq1} implies \(H^xK = HK\).

The second statement then follows easily: \[G = HH^x = H(H^x)^{x^{-1}} = HH = H.\]

1A.6: Permutation characters and Burnside's Lemma

Let \(G\) act on \(\Omega\), where both \(G\) and \(\Omega\) are finite. For each element \(g \in G\), write \(\chi(g) = \bars{\{\alpha \in \Omega \mid g \cdot \alpha = \alpha\}}\). The nonnegative-integer-valued function \(\chi\) is called the permutation character associated with the action. Show that

\begin{equation} \label{eq2} \tag{*} \sum_{g \in G} \chi(g) = \sum_{\alpha \in \Omega} \bars{G_\alpha} = n\bars{G}, \end{equation}

where \(n\) is the number of orbits of \(G\) on \(\Omega\).

The proof is clear when group actions are represented as tables. For example, here is the group action of \(S_3\) on the set \(\{1,2,3\}\), where all instances of elements being fixed are underlined:

  1 2 3
\(1\) 1 2 3
\((12)\) 2 1 3
\((13)\) 3 2 1
\((23)\) 1 3 2
\((123)\) 2 3 1
\((132)\) 3 1 2

The first sum in \eqref{eq2} corresponds to a horizontal summation of the underlined elements, while the second sum corresponds to a vertical summation. Lastly,

\begin{align*} \sum_{\alpha \in \Omega} \bars{G_\alpha} &= \sum_{\text{orbits}\ \mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega} \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{O}} \bars{G_\alpha} \\ &= \sum_{\text{orbits}\ \mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega} \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{O}} \frac{\bars{G}}{\bars{\mathcal{O}}} \\ &= \sum_{\text{orbits}\ \mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega} \bars{G} \\ &= n\bars{G}. \end{align*}

1A.7: Number of elements not lying in any conjugate of a proper subgroup

Let \(G\) be a finite group, and suppose that \(H < G\) is a proper subgroup. Show that the number of elements of \(G\) that do not lie in any conjugate of \(H\) is at least \(\bars{H}\).

Let \(G\) act on the left cosets of \(H\) by left multiplication. This action is clearly transitive, thus if \(\chi\) is the permutation character associated with the action, then \[\sum_{g \in G} \chi(g) = \bars{G}.\]

Now let \(H\) act similarly on left cosets of \(H\). Since \(H < G\), there is more than \(1\) left coset. But the orbit of the coset \(1H\) contains only itself, so there are at least \(2\) orbits. Thus \[\sum_{h \in H} \chi(h) \ge 2\bars{H}.\]

Note that \(g \in G\) fixes a coset \(xH\) i.e. \(gxH = xH\) iff \(x^{-1}gx \in H\) iff \(g \in H^x\). In other words, if \(g\) does not lie in any conjugate of \(H\), then \(\chi(g) = 0\). To find the number of elements \(g\) where \(\chi\) vanishes, observe that by combining the above two equations we get \[\sum_{g \not\in H} \chi(g) \le \bars{G} - 2\bars{H}.\]

But the number of elements \(g \not\in H\) is precisely \(\bars{G} - \bars{H}\), so there at least \(\bars{H}\) elements where \(\chi\) vanishes.

1A.8: McKay's proof of Cauchy's Theorem

Let \(G\) be a finite group, let \(n > 0\) be an integer. Let \(\Omega\) be the set of $n$-tuples \((x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n)\) of elements of \(G\) such that \(x_1x_2 \cdots x_n = 1\).

(a) Show that \(C_n\) acts on \(\Omega\) according to the formula \[k \cdot (x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n) = (x_{1+k},x_{2+k},\cdots,x_{n+k}),\] where \(k \in C_n\) and the subscripts are interpreted modulo \(n\).

If \(x_1x_2 \cdots x_n = 1\), then

\begin{align*} x_{1+k} x_{2+k} \cdots x_{n+k} &= ({x_k}^{-1} {x_{k-1}}^{-1} \cdots {x_1}^{-1}) (x_1 x_2 \cdots x_k)(x_{1+k} x_{2+k} \cdots x_n)(x_{n+1} x_{n+2} \cdots x_{n+k}) \\ &= ({x_k}^{-1} {x_{k-1}}^{-1} \cdots {x_1}^{-1}) (x_{n+1} x_{n+2} \cdots x_{n+k}) \\ &= 1, \end{align*}

so the action is well-defined. It is easy to check that the conditions for a group action are also satisfied.

(b) Now suppose that \(n=p\) is a prime number that divides \(\bars{G}\). Show that \(p\) divides the number of $C$-orbits of size \(1\) on \(\Omega\), and deduce that the number of elements of order \(p\) in \(G\) is congruent to \(-1\) mod \(p\).

Let \(x \in \Omega\). By the orbit-stabilizer theorem, \(\bars{\mathcal{O}_x} = \bars{C_n}/\bars{(C_n)_x}\) is either \(1\) or \(p\). Letting \(n_1\) and \(n_p\) be the number of size \(1\) and size \(p\) orbits respectively, we have \(n_1 + pn_p = \bars{\Omega}\). But \(p \mid \bars{G}^{p-1} = \bars{\Omega}\) so \(p \mid n_1\).

If the orbit of \((x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_p)\) has size \(1\), then \(x_1 = x_2 = \cdots = x_p\), so in particular \({x_1}^p = 1\). The number of elements such that \(x^p = 1\) is precisely the number of size \(1\) orbits \(n_1\), so the number of order \(p\) elements is \(n_1-1\) (as the identity satisfies \(x^p = 1\) but does not have order \(p\)). The fact that \(p \mid n_1\) gives the congruence.

1A.9: Groups of order \(pm\)

Suppose \(\bars{G} = pm\), where \(p > m\) and \(p\) is prime. Show that \(G\) has a unique subgroup of order \(p\).

Suppose \(G\) has distinct order \(p\) subgroups \(H\) and \(K\). Their intersection is trivial, thus the order of \(HK\) is \(p^2\). But this is impossible because then \(\bars{HK} = p^2 > pm = \bars{G}\).

1A.10: Index of subgroup in its normalizer

Let \(H \subseteq G\).

(a) Show that \(\bars{N_G(H) : H}\) is equal to the number of left cosets of \(H\) in \(G\) that are invariant under left multiplication by \(H\).

Let \(xH\) be any left coset. \(xH\) is fixed by all \(h \in H\) i.e. \(hxH = xH\) iff \(x^{-1}hx \in H\) iff \(h \in H^x\). But this means \(H \subseteq H^x\), so \(H = H^x\). Hence \(x \in N_G(H)\), and the statement follows.

(b) Suppose that \(\bars{H}\) is a $p$-power and that \(\bars{G : H}\) is divisible by \(p\). Show that \(\bars{N_G(H) : H}\) is divisible by \(p\).

Let \(\bars{H} = p^m\) and \(\bars{G : H} = pn\). Consider the action of \(H\) on the left cosets of \(H\) in \(G\) by left multiplication, and let \(n_k\) denote the number of size \(k\) orbits.

Note that the orbit sizes divide \(p^m\) and are thus $p$-powers themselves. Hence \[n_1 = pn - \sum_{i=1}^m p^i n_{p^i}\] is divisible by \(p\). But from part (a), we can see that \(\bars{N_G(H) : H}\) is precisely \(n_1\), completing the proof.

1B.1: Basic properties of Sylow $p$-subgroups

Let \(S \in \Syl_p(G)\), where \(G\) is a finite group.

(a) Let \(P \subseteq G\) be a $p$-subgroup. Show that \(PS\) is a subgroup if and only if \(P \subseteq S\).

Let \(\bars{G} = p^\alpha m\), \(\bars{P} = p^\beta\) and \(\bars{P \cap S} = p^\gamma\). If \(PS\) is a subgroup, then \[\bars{PS} = \frac{p^\alpha p^\beta}{p^\gamma}\] must divide \(p^\alpha m\). This forces \(p^\gamma = p^\beta\), or else \(S\) would not be a Sylow $p$-subgroup. This implies \(P \subseteq S\).

The reverse implication is trivial.

(b) If \(S \triangleleft G\), show that \(\Syl_p(G) = \{S\}\), and deduce that \(S\) is characteristic in \(G\).

Let \(H,K\) be subgroups of \(G\). If \(K \le N_G(H)\), then \(HK\) is a subgroup.

Let \(hk\) and \(h'k'\) be elements of \(HK\). Since \(K \le N_G(H)\), we also have \(kh'k^{-1} = h_0\) and \(k^{-1}h^{-1}k = h_1\) for some \(h_0,h_1 \in H\). Thus, \[hkh'k' = h(kh'k^{-1})kk' = hh_0kk' \in HK.\] Furthermore, \[(hk)^{-1} = k^{-1}h^{-1} = (k^{-1}h^{-1}k)k^{-1} = h_1k^{-1} \in HK.\]

Let \(\bars{S} = p^\alpha\). Suppose there was another Sylow $p$-subgroup \(S'\). Since \(S' \le N_G(S) = G\), \(SS'\) is a subgroup of \(G\). But (a) implies that \(S \subseteq S'\), a clear contradiction.

Lastly, each automorphism must send \(S\) to another Sylow $p$-subgroup i.e. itself, thus \(S\) is characteristic in \(G\).

1B.2: The subgroup \(O_p(G)\)

Show that \(O_p(G)\) (the intersection of the Sylow $p$-subgroups) is the unique largest normal $p$-subgroup of \(G\).

First, we show that \(O_p(G) \triangleleft G\). In fact, we can do one better and show that \(O_p(G)\) is characteristic in \(G\). Suppose there was an automorphism \(\varphi\) and an element \(g \in O_p(G)\) such that \(\varphi(g) \not\in O_p(G)\). This means \(\varphi(g)\) is not in some Sylow $p$-subgroup \(S\). But that implies \(g \not\in \varphi^{-1}(S)\), which cannot be true as \(\varphi^{-1}(S)\) is also a Sylow $p$-subgroup.

Secondly, we show that \(O_p(G)\) contains every normal $p$-subgroup.

Suppose \(H\) is a normal $p$-subgroup. For every Sylow $p$-subgroup \(S\) we have \(S \le N_G(H) = G\), so by the lemma in 1B.1 \(HS\) is a subgroup, implying \(H \subseteq S\). Hence \(H \subseteq O_p(G)\).

1B.3: Normalizer of a Sylow $p$-subgroup is self-normalizing

Let \(S \in \Syl_p(G)\), and write \(N = N_G(S)\). Show that \(N = N_G(N)\).

Note that \(S\) is a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(N\), and that \(S \triangleleft N\). Thus by 1B.1(b), \(S\) is characteristic in \(N\). Furthermore, \(N \triangleleft N_G(N)\), and this implies \(S \triangleleft N_G(N)\). But \(N_G(S)\) is the largest group \(K\) satisfying the property \(S \triangleleft K\), so \(N_G(N) \subseteq N_G(S)\) and equality follows.

1B.4: An alternative proof of the existence of Sylow $p$-subgroups

Let \(P \subseteq G\) be a $p$-subgroup such that \(\bars{G : P}\) is divisible by \(p\). Using Cauchy's theorem, but without appealing to Sylow's theorem, show that there exists a subgroup \(Q\) of \(G\) containing \(P\), and such that \(\bars{Q : P} = p\). Deduce that a maximal $p$-subgroup of \(G\) (which obviously must exist) must be a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\).

From 1A.10, we know that \(\bars{N_G(P) : P}\) is divisible by \(p\). By Cauchy's theorem, the quotient group \(N_G(P)/P\) has an order \(p\) subgroup \(\bar{Q}\). The corresponding subgroup \(Q \le G\) under the correspondence theorem then contains \(P\), and has order \(p\bars{P}\).

Let \(P \subset Q_1 \subset Q_2 \subset \cdots\) be the tower of subgroups constructed using this method. Since there is a maximum integer \(\alpha\) such that \(p^\alpha \mid \bars{G}\), this tower must stabilize to a maximal $p$-subgroup \(Q'\) of order \(p^\alpha\), which is then a Sylow $p$-subgroup.

1B.5: Basic facts about Hall $π$-subgroups, part 1

Let \(\pi\) be any set of prime numbers, and let \(\theta : G \rightarrow K\) be a surjective homomorphism of finite groups.

(a) If \(H\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(G\), prove that \(\theta(H)\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(K\).

Let \(N = \ker \theta\). Applying the first isomorphism theorem to \(\theta\) and \(\theta|_H\) respectively yields the equalities \[\bars{G} = \bars{N}\bars{K}\ \text{and}\ \bars{H} = \bars{H \cap N}\bars{\theta(H)}.\]

Since \(\bars{\theta(H)} \mid \bars{H}\), it is a $π$-subgroup. Furthermore, we have \[ \bars{K : \theta(H)} = \frac{\bars{K}\bars{H \cap N}}{\bars{H}} \mid \frac{\bars{K}\bars{N}}{\bars{H}} = \bars{G : H}, \] so for all \(p \in \pi\), \(p \not\mid \bars{K : \theta(H)}\) since \(p \not\mid \bars{G : H}\). Hence \(\theta(H)\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup.

(b) Show that every Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(K\) has the form \(\theta(H)\), where \(H\) is some Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\).

Let \(N = \ker \theta\). By the first isomorphism theorem, we can identify \(K\) with the quotient \(G/N\). If \(S\) is a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(K\), its preimage under \(\theta\) is a subgroup \(S' \le G\) with order \(\bars{S}\bars{N}\).

Let \(H\) be a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(S'\). Applying (a) to the map \(\theta|_{S'}\), we have that \(\theta(H)\) is a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(\theta(S') = S\). But \(S\) is already a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(K\), so \(\theta(H) = K\). Furthermore, we have that \(p \not\mid \bars{S' : H}\) and \[p \not\mid \bars{K : S} = \frac{\bars{G}}{\bars{N}\bars{S}} = \bars{G : S'},\] so \(p \not\mid \bars{G : H}\) and \(H\) is a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\).

(Note that the statement is not generally true if 'Sylow $p$-subgroup' is replaced with 'Hall $π$-subgroup', as a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(S'\) may not exist.)

(c) Show that \(\bars{\Syl_p(G)} \ge \bars{\Syl_p(K)}\) for every prime number \(p\).

This follows immediately from (b).

1B.6: Basic facts about Hall $π$-subgroups, part 2

Let \(G\) be a finite group, and let \(K \subseteq G\) be a subgroup. Suppose that \(H \subseteq G\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup, where \(\pi\) is some set of primes. Show that if \(HK\) is a subgroup, then \(H \cap K\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(K\).

Clearly \(H \cap K\) is a $π$-subgroup of \(K\). Furthermore, the fact that \(HK\) is a subgroup implies \[\bars{K : H \cap K} = \frac{\bars{HK}}{\bars{H}} \mid \bars{G : H}.\] Thus \(p \not\mid \bars{K : H \cap K}\) for all \(p \in \pi\), since \(p \not\mid \bars{G : H}\). So \(H \cap K\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(K\).

1B.7: The subgroup \(O_\pi(G)\)

Let \(G\) be a finite group, and let \(\pi\) be any set of primes.

(a) Show that \(G\) has a (necessarily unique) normal $π$-subgroup \(N\) such that \(M \subseteq N\) whenever \(M \triangleleft G\) is a $π$-subgroup.

Let \(M_1, M_2, \cdots, M_n\) be all the normal $π$-subgroups of \(G\), and let \(N\) be the composite \(M_1M_2 \cdots M_n\). By the lemma in 1B.1, it is a subgroup; in fact it is easily seen to be a $π$-subgroup. Also it contains all \(M_i\) and is normal in \(G\) itself, since \[g \left(\prod_i m_i\right) g^{-1} = \prod_i gm_ig^{-1} \in N\] for \(m_i \in M_i\).

(b) Show that the subgroup \(N\) of part (a) is contained in every Hall $π$-subgroup of \(G\).

If \(H\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup, then \(HN\) is a subgroup by the lemma in 1B.1. By 1B.6, \(H \cap N\) is a Hall $π$-subgroup of \(N\). But \(N\) is a $π$-subgroup itself, so \(H \cap N = N\) i.e. \(N \subseteq H\).

(c) Assuming that \(G\) has a Hall $π$-subgroup, show that \(N\) is exactly the intersection of all the Hall $π$-subgroups of \(G\).

The intersection of the Hall $π$-subgroups is a characteristic and hence normal $π$-subgroup of \(G\). Hence by part (a), it is contained in \(N\). The reverse inclusion was proved in part (b).

1B.8: The subgroup \(O^\pi(G)\)

Let \(G\) be a finite group, and let \(\pi\) be any set of primes.

(a) Show that \(G\) has a (necessarily unique) normal subgroup \(N\) such that \(G/N\) is a $π$-group and \(N \subseteq M\) whenever \(M \triangleleft G\) and \(G/M\) is a $π$-group.

Let \(M_1, M_2, \cdots, M_n\) be all the normal subgroups where the corresponding quotients are $π$-groups, and let \(N = \cap_i M_i\). Clearly \(N \triangleleft G\) and \(N\) is contained in all \(M_i\); furthermore by considering the homomorphism \[g \mapsto (gM_1, gM_2, \cdots, gM_n),\] one can see that \(G/N\) is a subgroup of \(\prod_i G/M_i\). Since all primes dividing \(\bars{\prod_i G/M_i} = \prod_i \bars{G : M_i}\) are contained in \(\pi\), it follows that \(G/N\) is a $π$-group.

(b) Show that the subgroup \(N\) of part (a) is generated by the set of all elements of \(G\) that have order not divisible by any prime in \(\pi\).

Let \(\pi = \{p_1,p_2,\cdots,p_k\}\), and let \(\pi' = \{p_{k+1},p_{k+2},\cdots,p_l\}\) be the remaining prime divisors of \(\bars{G}\). Let \(n_i\) denote the multiplicity of \(p_i\) in \(\bars{G}\) for \(1 \le i \le l\), and let \(d = \prod_{i=k+1}^l {p_i}^{n_i}\).

Let \(A = \{g \in G \mid g^d = 1\}\). For any \(g \in G\), \(g \in A\) if and only if its order is not divisible by any prime in \(\pi\). Thus \(A\) is equal to the set in the question, and it suffices to show that \(\angles{A} = N\).

Suppose \(g \in A\). Then by the first isomorphism theorem, the image of the subgroup \(\angles{g}\) under the projection map \(\theta : G \rightarrow G/N\) satisfies \[\bars{\theta\angles{g}} = \frac{\bars{\angles{g}}}{\bars{\angles{g} \cap N}}.\]

Since \(G/N\) is a $π$-group, \(\theta\angles{g}\) is a $π$-group as well. But the prime divisors of \(\bars{\angles{g}}\) all lie in \(\pi'\), forcing \[\bars{\angles{g} \cap N} = \bars{\angles{g}}\] which means \(\angles{g} \subseteq N\). Since \(g \in A\) was arbitrary, we then have \(\angles{A} \subseteq N\).

To prove the reverse inclusion, we have to show that \(\angles{A}\) satisfies the hypotheses in part (a). Firstly, note that \[a^d = 1\ \text{implies}\ (gag^{-1})^d = ga^dg^{-1} = 1,\]

so \(gag^{-1} \in A\) for any \(g \in G\) and \(a \in A\). Now, every \(x \in \angles{A}\) can be written as a finite product \(\prod_i x_i\) where for each \(x_i\), either \(x_i \in A\) or \(x_i^{-1} \in A\). For all \(g \in G\), we have \[gxg^{-1} = \prod_i gx_ig^{-1}.\]

If \(x_i \in A\), then \(gx_ig^{-1} \in A\) as shown above. Otherwise \((gx_ig^{-1})^{-1} = gx_i^{-1}g^{-1} \in A\) so \(gx_ig^{-1} \in \angles{A}\). Hence \(gxg^{-1} \in \angles{A}\), implying \(\angles{A} \triangleleft G\).

Furthermore, let \(S\) be a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\), where \(p \in \pi'\). Then the order of its elements are $p$-powers which divide \(d\), so \(S \subseteq \angles{A}\). This means that \(d\) must divide \(\bars{\angles{A}}\), since otherwise \(\angles{A}\) would not contain some Sylow $p$-subgroup. Thus \(G/\angles{A}\) is a $π$-group, and by part (a) \(N \subseteq \angles{A}\).

1C.1: Subgroups containing Sylow $p$-subgroup's normalizer are self-normalizing

Let \(P \in \Syl_p(G)\), and suppose that \(N_G(P) \subseteq H \subseteq G\), where \(H\) is a subgroup. Prove that \(H = N_G(H)\) (this generalizes 1B.3).

Clearly \(H \triangleleft N_G(H)\) and \(P\) is a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(H\). Hence by the Frattini argument, \(N_G(H) = N_G(P)H = H\).

1C.2: Sylow $p$-subgroups of a subgroup

Let \(H \subseteq G\), where \(G\) is a finite group.

(a) If \(P \in \Syl_p(H)\), prove that \(P = H \cap S\) for some member \(S \in \Syl_p(G)\).

\(P\) is a $p$-subgroup of \(G\), so it is contained in some Sylow $p$-subgroup \(S\) of \(G\). Let \(\bars{H} = p^\alpha m\) and \(\bars{S} = p^\beta\), where \(p \not\mid m\) and \(\beta \ge \alpha\). Then \(\bars{H \cap S}\) is a $p$-power dividing \(p^\alpha\). But \(P \subseteq H \cap S\) forces \(\bars{H \cap S} = \bars{P} = p^\alpha\), so \(P = H \cap S\).

(b) Show that \(n_p(H) \le n_p(G)\) for all primes \(p\).

This follows immediately from (a).

1C.3: Subset of all $p$-power-order elements

Let \(G\) be a finite group, and let \(X\) be the subset of \(G\) consisting of all elements whose order is a power of \(p\), where \(p\) is some fixed prime.

(a) Show that \(X = \cup\ \Syl_p(G)\).

If \(x \in X\) has $p$-power order, then the subgroup \(\angles{x}\) is contained in some Sylow $p$-subgroup \(S\). In particular \(x \in S \subseteq \Syl_p(G)\). Conversely, all elements of a Sylow $p$-subgroup have $p$-power order, so \(\Syl_p(G) \subseteq X\).

(b) Show that if \(p\) divides \(\bars{G}\), then \(\bars{X}\) is divisible by \(p\).

The intersection of any number of Sylow $p$-subgroups has $p$-power order. Thus by the inclusion-exclusion principle, \(\bars{X}\) is divisible by \(p\).

1C.4: Order \(120\) subgroup contains index \(3\) or \(5\) subgroup

Let \(\bars{G} = 120 = 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5\). Show that \(G\) has a subgroup of index \(3\) or a subgroup of index \(5\) (or both).

We know that \(n_2 \equiv 1\) mod \(p\) and \(n_2 \mid 15\), so the possibilities are \(1\), \(3\), \(5\) and \(15\).

  1. \(n_2 = 1\)

    The unique Sylow $2$-subgroup \(G_2\) is normal in \(G\). If \(G_3 \in \Syl_3(G)\) and \(G_5 \in \Syl_5(G)\), then by this previous lemma \(G_2G_3\) and \(G_2G_5\) are subgroups. Since \(G_2 \cap G_3\) and \(G_2 \cap G_5\) are trivial, the subgroups' indices are \(5\) and \(3\) respectively.

  2. \(n_2 = 3\)

    If \(G_2 \in \Syl_2(G)\), the subgroup \(N_G(G_2)\) has index \(3\).

  3. \(n_2 = 5\)

    If \(G_2 \in \Syl_2(G)\), the subgroup \(N_G(G_2)\) has index \(5\).

  4. \(n_2 = 15\)

    Let \(G_2 \in \Syl_2(G)\). Since \(n_2 \not\equiv 1\) mod \(2^2\), the largest intersection between any two Sylow $2$-subgroups has order \(2\). Thus the number of distinct elements in Sylow $2$-subgroups is at least \[2 + 15(8-2) = 92.\]

    Suppose that \(n_3,n_5 \neq 1\), forcing \(n_3 \ge 4\) and \(n_5 = 6\). Then the number of distinct elements in Sylow $3$- and $5$-subgroups, excluding the identity, is at least \[4(3-1) + 6(5-1) = 32.\] But that implies there are at least \(92+32 = 124 > 120\) distinct elements, which cannot be true. Hence at least one of \(n_3,n_5\) must be \(1\). If \(n_3 = 1\), then the Sylow $3$-subgroup \(G_3\) is normal, and \(G_2G_3\) is an index 5 subgroup (likewise if \(n_5 = 1\)).

1C.5: Normalizer of Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(A_{p+1}\)

Let \(P \in \Syl_p(G)\), where \(G = A_{p+1}\), the alternating group on \(p+1\) symbols. Show that \(\bars{N_G(P)} = p(p-1)/2\).

If \(p = 2\) then the statement is trivially true. Hence we shall only consider odd \(p\).

The order of an element of \(A_{p+1}\) is the LCM of the lengths of cycles in its cycle decomposition. Since the maximum cycle length is \(p\) (a (\(p+1\))-cycle is an odd permutation and is not contained in \(A_{p+1}\)), it follows that the maximum $p$-power order is \(p\) itself. Thus by 1C.3(a), the union of Sylow $p$-subgroups is precisely the set of all order \(p\) elements, together with the identity.

The order \(p\) elements are exactly the $p$-cycles of \(A_{p+1}\). By this formula, the number of order \(p\) elements is \[\frac{(p+1)!}{p[(p+1)-p]!} = (p+1)(p-1)!\]

On the other hand, the number of distinct elements in all Sylow $p$-subgroups, excluding the identity, is \(n_2(p-1)\). Since the two expressions are equal, we have \(n_2 = (p+1)(p-2)!\), so \[\bars{N_G(P)} = \frac{(p+1)!}{2(p+1)(p-2)!} = \frac{p(p-1)}{2}.\]

1C.6: Sylow $p$-subgroups in composite groups

Let \(G = HK\), where \(H\) and \(K\) are subgroups, and fix a prime \(p\).

(a) Show that there exists \(P \in \Syl_p(G)\) such that \(P \cap H \in \Syl_p(H)\) and \(P \cap K \in \Syl_p(K)\).

By the Sylow C-theorem, there is some \(Q \in \Syl_p(G)\) and \(g = hk \in G\) such that \(Q^{hk} \cap H \in \Syl_p(H)\) and \(Q \cap K \in \Syl_p(K)\). This implies \[Q^k \cap H = (Q^{hk} \cap H)^{h^{-1}} \in \Syl_p(H)\] and \[Q^k \cap K = (Q \cap K)^k \in \Syl_p(K).\]

(b) If \(P\) is as in (a), show that \(P = (P \cap H)(P \cap K)\).

Clearly \((P \cap H)(P \cap K) \subseteq P\), so it suffices to prove that \[ \bars{P} \le \bars{(P \cap H)(P \cap K)} = \frac{\bars{P \cap H}\bars{P \cap K}}{\bars{P \cap H \cap K}}. \]

Let \(\bars{H} = p^\alpha l\), \(\bars{K} = p^\beta m\) and \(\bars{G} = p^\gamma n\), where \(p \not\mid l,m,n\) (hence \(\bars{P} = p^\gamma\)). Then by (a), \(\bars{P \cap H} = p^\alpha\) and \(\bars{P \cap K} = p^\beta\). Furthermore, we have \[ \bars{G} = p^\gamma n = \frac{p^{\alpha+\beta}lm}{\bars{H \cap K}} = \frac{\bars{H}\bars{K}}{\bars{H \cap K}}. \]

Since \(\gamma\) is the exponent of \(p\) in \(\bars{G}\), it follows that the exponent of \(p\) in \(\bars{H \cap K}\) is \(p^{\alpha+\beta-\gamma}\). Since \(P \cap H \cap K\) is a $p$-group, \(\bars{P \cap H \cap K} \mid p^{\alpha+\beta-\gamma}\). Putting it all together, \[ \bars{P} = p^\gamma = \frac{p^\alpha p^\beta}{p^{\alpha+\beta-\gamma}} \mid \frac{\bars{P \cap H}\bars{P \cap K}}{\bars{P \cap H \cap K}}. \] This completes the proof.

1C.7: Group where every maximal subgroup has prime index

Let \(G\) be a finite group in which every maximal subgroup has prime index, and let \(p\) be the largest prime divisor of \(\bars{G}\). Show that a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\) is normal.

Let \(P \in \Syl_p(G)\). If \(N_G(P) \neq G\), then it is contained in some maximal subgroup \(M\), and it follows that \(N_G(P) = N_M(P)\). Thus \[n_p(M) = \bars{M : N_M(P)} \mid \bars{G : N_G(P)} = n_p(G).\]

In particular, \(n_p(G)/n_p(M)\) is a prime \(p'\) since \(M\) has prime index. Since \(p\) does not divide \(n_p(G)\) we have \(p' \neq p\), which by hypothesis implies \(p' < p\). At the same time, \(n_p(M)\) and \(n_p(G)\) are congruent to \(1\) mod \(p\). This means that \[n_p(G) = p'n_p(M) \equiv p'\ \text{mod $p$}.\]

But then this means \(p' = 1\) since \(p' < p\), which is a contradiction. Hence \(N_G(P) = G\).

1C.8: Number of order \(p^a\) subgroups in \(G\) and its Sylow $p$-subgroups

Let \(P\) be a Sylow $p$-subgroup of \(G\). Show that for every nonnegative integer \(a\), the numbers of subgroups of order \(p^a\) in \(P\) and in \(G\) are congruent mod \(p\).

Let \(P\) act on the order \(p^a\) subgroups of \(G\) by conjugation. This action has two blocks: one consists of subgroups contained in \(P\), and the other consists of the remaining subgroups. For given \(p \in P\) and a subgroup \(P_1 \le P\), \({P_1}^p\) is also a subgroup of \(P\). Conversely, if \(H^p \le P\), then \(H = (H^p)^{p^{-1}} \le P\).

Let \(k_1,k_2\) denote the number of elements in the respective block, and suppose \(k_2\) is not divisible by \(p\). Since all orbit sizes are $p$-powers, it follows that there is some size \(1\) orbit \(\{Q\}\). \(Q\) is invariant under conjugation by \(P\), so \(P \le N_G(Q)\) and \(PQ\) is a subgroup. But by 1B.1(a), this implies that \(Q \le P\), which contradicts the fact that \(Q\) lies in the second block. Therefore, \(k_1+k_2 \equiv k_1\) mod \(p\), and this completes the proof.

1D.1: Normaliser of Sylow $p$-subgroup in a subgroup

Let \(P \in \Syl_p(H)\), where \(H \subseteq G\), and suppose that \(N_G(P) \subseteq H\). Show that \(p\) does not divide \(\bars{G : H}\).

Suppose \(p \mid \bars{G : H}\). Then by 1A.10(b), \(N_G(P)/P\) is divisible by \(p\). This means that \(N_G(P) \not\subseteq H\), since the order of \(P\) is a maximal $p$-power of \(\bars{H}\).

1D.3: Frobenius complements

Let \(H \subseteq G\) have the property that \(H \cap H^g = 1\) for all elements \(g \in G-H\).

(a) Show that \(N_G(K) \subseteq H\) for all subgroups \(K\) with \(1 < K \subseteq H\).

For all \(g \in G-H\), we have \(H \cap K^g \subseteq H \cap H^g = 1\). Thus \(K^g \not\subseteq H\), forcing \(N_G(K) \subseteq H\).

(b) Show that \(H\) is a Hall subgroup of \(G\).

Let \(H\) act on the left cosets of \(H\) in \(G\). The stabiliser of the coset \(1H\) is \(H\) itself, thus its orbit has size \(1\). On the other hand, the stabiliser of the coset \(xH\) where \(x \not\in H\) is given by \(H \cap H^x\): \[hxH = xH \Leftrightarrow x^{-1}hx \in H \Leftrightarrow h \in H^x.\]

Since \(H \cap H^x = 1\), the orbit has size \(\bars{H}\). Adding up the orbit sizes yields \(\bars{G : H} = 1 + k\bars{H}\) for some nonnegative integer \(k\), which shows that \(\bars{G : H}\) and \(\bars{H}\) are coprime.
